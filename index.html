Agriculture

Agriculture

Impact: 9/10. Drives 75-96% of tropical deforestation.¹ Cattle (41%) & oilseeds (18%) lead.²
Risk: 9/10. High physical, regulatory (EUDR), and market risks.³
Food & Bev

Food & Beverage

Impact: 8/10 (Indirect). Demand for soy (77% for feed)⁴ & palm oil (~50% of products)⁵.
Risk: 9/10. High supply chain, reputational, and EUDR compliance risks.³
Automotive

Automotive

Impact: 6/10. Major driver via leather (~50% of Brazil exports)⁶ & rubber (4M ha deforestation).⁷
Risk: 7/10. High supply chain risk; directly targeted by EUDR.
Personal Care

Personal Care & Cosmetics

Impact: 3/10. Small volume driver (~2% cosmetics, 3% personal care of palm oil).⁸
Risk: 8/10. Extreme reputational risk and direct EUDR impact.
Pharmaceutical

Pharmaceutical

Impact: 1/10. Negligible driver.
Risk: 8/10. Existential R&D risk. 20% of medicinal plants at risk.⁹ Losing one potential drug every two years.¹⁰
Software & IT

Software & IT Services

Impact: 1/10. Land use impact is negligible. Primary environmental focus is on energy consumption and e-waste, not deforestation.¹¹
Risk: 2/10. Low direct risk. Indirect risks from climate change affecting infrastructure.
Mining

Mining

Impact: 7/10. A major driver. Mining concessions overlapped with 8% of intact forests (2000-2013).¹²
Risk: 4/10. Primary risks are regulatory and reputational, not operational risks from ecosystem collapse.
Unsustainable Logging

Unsustainable Logging

Impact: 8/10. Illegal logging is a major industry, accounting for 15-30% of the global wood trade.¹³
Risk: 3/10. Business model relies on forest removal. Faces high regulatory risk but low operational risk from biodiversity loss.

Reference List

  1. Geist, H. J., & Lambin, E. F. (2002). Proximate Causes and Underlying Driving Forces of Tropical Deforestation. BioScience.

    Methodology: A meta-analysis of 152 subnational case studies to quantify the frequency of drivers. Agricultural expansion was a proximate cause in 96% of cases.

  2. Pendrill, F., et al. (2019). Agricultural and forestry trade drives large share of tropical deforestation emissions. Global Environmental Change.

    Methodology: Quantifies where deforestation occurs from agriculture/forestry expansion and what products are grown on this land (2005-2013).

  3. European Commission. (2023). Regulation (EU) 2023/1115...

    Methodology: A legal document establishing regulatory risk. It mandates due diligence for cattle, cocoa, coffee, palm oil, rubber, soy, and wood to prove deforestation-free supply chains.

  4. Poore, J., & Nemecek, T. (2018). Reducing food's environmental impacts... Science.

    Methodology: Meta-analysis of 570 life-cycle assessments (38,700 farms, 119 countries) to quantify food's environmental impacts.

  5. World Wildlife Fund (WWF). "8 Things to Know About Palm Oil".

    Methodology: Based on industry analysis and supply chain tracking, this statistic reflects the ubiquity of palm oil in processed supermarket foods.

  6. Lead The Charge. (2024). "The Road to a Deforestation-Free Auto Supply Chain".

    Methodology: Analysis of trade data and supply chain investigations linking leather exports from deforestation hotspots to the auto sector.

  7. Lead The Charge. (2024). "The Road to a Deforestation-Free Auto Supply Chain".

    Methodology: Analysis of land-use change data and commodity mapping to attribute forest loss to rubber plantation expansion since 1993.

  8. The Cosmetic, Toiletry and Perfumery Association (CTPA). "Palm Oil".

    Methodology: Industry analysis of global palm oil production volumes and consumption data by end-use sector.

  9. Ecologi. (2023). "Industries and sectors driving deforestation...".

    Methodology: A synthesis of data from various environmental reports on known medicinal plants and the percentage threatened by habitat loss.

  10. Cox, P. A., & Balick, M. J. (1994). The Ethnobotanical Approach to Drug Discovery. Scientific American.

    Methodology: An estimate based on the rate of biodiversity loss and historical frequency of discovering novel compounds from natural sources.

  11. Avellán, T. (2020). The digital tech industry and the climate crisis. Nature Climate Change.

    Methodology: A commentary synthesizing research on the environmental impact of the digital technology sector, highlighting that impacts are concentrated in the life cycle of hardware (e-waste) and energy consumption of data centers, not direct land use change.

  12. Sonter, L. J., et al. (2017). Mining drives extensive deforestation in the Brazilian Amazon. Science Advances.

    Methodology: This study used satellite data to quantify deforestation caused directly by mining leases and indirectly by infrastructure (e.g., roads, railways) supporting mining operations in the Brazilian Amazon, finding mining-related deforestation is 12 times greater than what occurs inside mine leases alone.

  13. INTERPOL. (n.d.). Forestry crime.

    Methodology: Based on analysis of global crime data and market intelligence, INTERPOL estimates that illegal logging comprises 15-30% of the global legal timber trade, representing a major direct driver of deforestation and forest degradation.
